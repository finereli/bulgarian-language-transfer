# Course Writing Principles

Extracted from the Language Transfer Thinking Method Guidebook by
Mihalis Eleftheriou (`docs/thinking-method-guidebook.pdf`), adapted for
this text-based Bulgarian course. Use these when writing or reviewing
any lesson content.

## 1. One thought at a time

What feels like "one thing" to the course writer is often four or five
separate thoughts to the learner. Before teaching something, list every
constituent idea the learner needs. Teach each one separately, with
practice, before combining them.

**Example (verb conjugation):** "to be" in the present has six forms.
Each pronoun + verb-form pair is a separate thought. Don't list them in
a table - introduce one, practice it, then introduce the next, weaving
the earlier ones into the new exercises.

**Even when the verb form is the same**, the pronouns are new. той е,
тя е, то е share the same verb form (е), but три/тя/то are three new
words. Introduce той е first with practice, then тя е separately - the
learner only needs to absorb one new pronoun at a time.

**Test:** if an exercise requires two things the learner hasn't
individually practiced yet, you've skipped a thought.

## 2. No vertical lists

The guidebook's strongest structural warning. "We don't run through
lists of vertical content like most courses do." Conjugation tables,
pronoun lists, preposition inventories - these are vertical. Language
doesn't work vertically; sentences are built horizontally from pieces
across different areas. A list of six forms presented at once becomes a
memorization task, which is the opposite of the method.

**Instead:** spread the forms across exercises. Each form gets its own
introduction + practice. Only show the full picture as a recap *after*
the learner has already used every piece.

## 3. Manage cognitive load through tension contours

Don't aim for a flat difficulty curve. Aim for waves - tension rises
when a new idea is introduced, falls when the learner practices and
gets comfortable, rises again with the next idea. Like music.

When cognitive load is too high (too much new at once, or
insufficient groundwork), learners enter "panic mode" - they abandon
thinking and start memorizing or guessing. When it's too low, they
get bored and disengage.

**Practical rules:**
- After introducing something new, give 2-3 exercises before adding
  the next new element.
- Mix in vocabulary and structures from earlier lessons ("weaving")
  to create variety without new cognitive load.
- A note that introduces two new ideas should probably be two notes
  with practice between them.

## 4. Weave, don't stack

Weaving means the order of presentation is driven by what connects
naturally, not by what belongs together in a grammar textbook.
Information about a topic gets scattered across the course, pre-seeded
before it's formally taught, revisited after.

**For exercises:** when practicing a new form, build sentences using
vocabulary and structures already learned. This gives masked
repetition of old material while the learner's focus is on the new
thing.

**For notes:** when introducing, say, plural pronouns, use them in
sentences that recycle known vocabulary (турист, студент, доктор)
rather than introducing new words at the same time.

## 5. Mask repetition

The learner needs lots of practice, but it shouldn't feel repetitive.
Vary the surface while keeping the underlying practice the same:

- Change the vocabulary while keeping the structure.
- Change the structure while keeping the vocabulary.
- Shift the focus: "Say X" then later "Now say X without the pronoun."
- Use the `after` field to affirm process, not just give the answer:
  "Good - you picked the right form of 'to be' for 'he'."

Overt repetition causes the learner to shortcut the thinking process
and just recall the answer pattern. Masked repetition keeps the
thinking active.

## 6. Import knowledge from the base language

The learner already speaks English (and in our case, likely Hebrew
and possibly Russian). Use these as bridges:

- Hebrew drops "to be" in the present - Bulgarian doesn't. Name it.
- English word order in questions uses inversion - Bulgarian uses ли.
- Russian speakers can lean on shared vocabulary but must watch for
  false friends.

The `ru` field exists for Russian-specific bridges. The main text should
build from English and Hebrew. Don't describe the target language in
abstract grammatical terms when you can point to something the
learner already does in a language they speak.

## 7. Cue, don't describe

The guidebook distinguishes between "key information" (needed to
build sentences) and "supporting information" (interesting but not
essential). Cue the difference through tone and placement.

For our text format this means:
- The `body` of a note should focus on the one thing being taught.
- Extra color goes in `after` fields on exercises, or in `ru` notes.
- Don't explain everything the language does with a feature - just
  enough to build the next set of sentences. More can be woven in
  later.

## 8. Exercises are for thinking, not testing

Frame exercises as invitations to think through the language, not as
quizzes. The prompt wording matters:

- "How would you say..." is better than "Translate:"
- "What do you think X would be?" invites construction.
- The `after` field should validate the *process*, not just confirm the
  answer.

If the learner gets it wrong, that's the method working. The AI
feedback feature handles correction; the course structure should make
the right answer *discoverable* through thinking.

## 9. Correct through process, not answers

When the `after` field follows an exercise, use it to reinforce the
thinking process rather than just restating the answer:

- Bad: "The answer is Ти си студент."
- Good: "You matched 'you' to ти and picked си - the 'to be' form
  that goes with ти."

This also means designing exercise sequences where each exercise
builds on the previous one, so a mistake in exercise 2 is naturally
corrected by the approach required for exercise 3.

## 10. Practical sequence for introducing a paradigm

When a new conjugation, declension, or set of related forms needs to
be taught:

1. **Introduce the first form** with a clear example sentence and a
   connection to something the learner knows.
2. **Practice it** with 2-3 exercises using known vocabulary.
3. **Introduce the next form** - ideally one that contrasts with the
   first in a way that makes both clearer.
4. **Practice it** with 2-3 exercises, weaving in the first form.
5. **Continue** until all forms are covered.
6. **Mix** with exercises that require choosing between forms.
7. **Recap** with a summary note (the "table" view) - but only now,
   after the learner has already used everything in it.

This is slower than a table. That's the point. The learner arrives at
the table already knowing what's in it, so the table is a satisfying
confirmation rather than an overwhelming dump.
